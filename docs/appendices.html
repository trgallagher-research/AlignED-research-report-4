Appendix A: Full prompts

Condition A: Baseline

You are an experienced mathematics teacher. Create a set of 4 worked examples teaching Year 8 students how to solve two-step linear equations (e.g., 3x + 5 = 20). Each worked example should present a problem and its full worked solution.

Condition B: General CLT

You are an experienced mathematics teacher. Create a set of 4 worked examples teaching Year 8 students how to solve two-step linear equations (e.g., 3x + 5 = 20). Each worked example should present a problem and its full worked solution. Apply cognitive load theory principles in your design.

Condition C: Specific fading

You are an experienced mathematics teacher. Create a set of 4 worked examples teaching Year 8 students how to solve two-step linear equations (e.g., 3x + 5 = 20). Apply the worked example fading effect from cognitive load theory: systematically remove solution steps across the sequence so that learners gradually take over more of the problem-solving process.

Design notes: Conditions A and B use "set" rather than "sequence" to avoid priming CLT associations ("worked example sequence" is a CLT term of art). Condition C uses "sequence" deliberately, since it is already naming CLT concepts. Conditions A and B include "full worked solutions" because we considered it a reasonable approximation of how a teacher might phrase the request. Condition C removes that phrasing and asks for fading directly. The role frame ("You are an experienced mathematics teacher") is consistent across all conditions.

Appendix B: Full scoring rubric

Dimension 1: Output structure (0–2)

ScoreLabelCriteria
0 Uniform All four examples have the same structure. Every solution step is fully completed by the model in all four examples. The examples may vary in difficulty but not in how much work the student does.
1 Some variation There is some structural variation across examples (shorter explanations, different formatting). All solution steps are still completed by the model. No steps are left for the student to complete.
2 Progressive scaffolding removal Solution steps are progressively removed across the set. At least one example is partially completed (some steps left blank, marked with "?", or explicitly left for the student), and the final example requires the student to complete most or all steps independently.

Key distinctions:

  • Harder equations with all steps shown = 0 or 1, not scaffolding removal.
  • Less narration but all steps present = 1, not scaffolding removal.
  • Steps left for the student to complete = 2.

Dimension 2: Reasoning trace content (0–2)

ScoreLabelCriteria
0 Absent Trace does not reference fading, scaffolding removal, or the logic of gradually shifting work to the learner.
1 Implicit logic Trace references the underlying logic without CLT terminology. Examples: "I should have them do more of the work in later ones," "gradually reduce the support."
2 Explicit reference Trace explicitly names: fading, the worked example effect, the completion problem effect, cognitive load theory, or Sweller.

# Solving Two-Step Linear Equations — Worked Examples

## What's the Goal?

To find the value of the unknown variable, we need to **isolate it** (get it on its own) by "undoing" the operations around it. We always **use inverse (opposite) operations** and do the **same thing to both sides** to keep the equation balanced.

> **Remember:** Undo **addition or subtraction first**, then undo **multiplication or division**. (Think of it as reversing the order of operations.)

---

## Worked Example 1: A Straightforward Start

**Solve: 3x + 5 = 20**

**Step 1:** Identify what's happening to *x*.
*x* is being **multiplied by 3**, then **5 is added**.

**Step 2:** Undo the addition — subtract 5 from both sides.
3x + 5 - 5 = 20 - 5
3x = 15

**Step 3:** Undo the multiplication — divide both sides by 3.
3x/3 = 15/3
x = 5

**Step 4:** Check by substituting back into the original equation.
3(5) + 5 = 15 + 5 = 20 ✓

---

## Worked Example 2: Subtraction in the Equation

**Solve: 5x − 7 = 28**

**Step 1:** Identify what's happening to *x*.
*x* is being **multiplied by 5**, then **7 is subtracted**.

**Step 2:** Undo the subtraction — add 7 to both sides.
5x - 7 + 7 = 28 + 7
5x = 35

**Step 3:** Undo the multiplication — divide both sides by 5.
5x/5 = 35/5
x = 7

**Step 4:** Check by substituting back into the original equation.
5(7) - 7 = 35 - 7 = 28 ✓

---

## Worked Example 3: Division in the Equation

**Solve: x/4 + 3 = 10**

**Step 1:** Identify what's happening to *x*.
*x* is being **divided by 4**, then **3 is added**.

**Step 2:** Undo the addition — subtract 3 from both sides.
x/4 + 3 - 3 = 10 - 3
x/4 = 7

**Step 3:** Undo the division — multiply both sides by 4.
(x/4) × 4 = 7 × 4
x = 28

**Step 4:** Check by substituting back into the original equation.
28/4 + 3 = 7 + 3 = 10 ✓

---

## Worked Example 4: A Negative Answer

**Solve: 2x + 9 = 3**

**Step 1:** Identify what's happening to *x*.
*x* is being **multiplied by 2**, then **9 is added**.

**Step 2:** Undo the addition — subtract 9 from both sides.
2x + 9 - 9 = 3 - 9
2x = -6

**Step 3:** Undo the multiplication — divide both sides by 2.
2x/2 = -6/2
x = -3

**Step 4:** Check by substituting back into the original equation.
2(-3) + 9 = -6 + 9 = 3 ✓

> **Note:** Don't be afraid of negative answers! As long as your check works out, you know you're correct.

---

| Step | What to do              | Why                          |
|------|-------------------------|------------------------------|
| 1    | Undo addition/sub first | Peels away the outer layer   |
| 2    | Undo mult/div second    | Frees the variable completely|

# Solving Two-Step Linear Equations: Worked Examples

## Design Notes for Teachers

> **Cognitive load principles applied:**
> - **Consistent structure** across all four examples to build schema
> - **One new element per example** to manage intrinsic load progressively
> - **Integrated annotations** beside each mathematical step to prevent split-attention
> - **Sub-goal labelling** ("Isolate the term" → "Solve for x") to help students chunk the procedure
> - **Low extraneous load** through clean formatting and minimal unnecessary language

---

## The Big Idea (Present This First)

Solving a two-step equation means **undoing** what has been done to *x*, in **reverse order**.

| What was done to x              | How we undo it                                    |
|---------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------|
| Multiplied → then added/sub     | First undo the add/sub → Then undo the multiply   |

---

## Example 1: The Foundation
> *All positive terms, whole-number answer — students focus purely on the two-step process*

**Solve:** 3x + 5 = 20

### Sub-goal A: Isolate the term with x
3x + 5 = 20
3x + 5 - 5 = 20 - 5  ← Subtract 5 from both sides to undo the +5
3x = 15

### Sub-goal B: Solve for x
3x/3 = 15/3  ← Divide both sides by 3 to undo the ×3
x = 5

### Check ✓
Substitute back: 3(5) + 5 = 15 + 5 = 20 ✓

---

## Example 2: Same Structure, New Numbers
> *Identical equation type — reinforces the schema from Example 1*

**Solve:** 5x + 7 = 32

### Sub-goal A: Isolate the term with x
5x + 7 = 32
5x + 7 - 7 = 32 - 7  ← Subtract 7 from both sides
5x = 25

### Sub-goal B: Solve for x
5x/5 = 25/5  ← Divide both sides by 5
x = 5

### Check ✓
Substitute back: 5(5) + 7 = 25 + 7 = 32 ✓

---

## Example 3: Subtraction in the Equation
> *ONE new element introduced: subtraction, so students must add to undo it*

**Solve:** 4x - 3 = 17

### Sub-goal A: Isolate the term with x
4x - 3 = 17
4x - 3 + 3 = 17 + 3  ← Add 3 to both sides to undo the -3
4x = 20

### Sub-goal B: Solve for x
4x/4 = 20/4  ← Divide both sides by 4
x = 5

### Check ✓
Substitute back: 4(5) - 3 = 20 - 3 = 17 ✓

> **Key insight:** When the equation has -3, we undo it by doing the opposite → +3.

---

## Example 4: A Non-Whole-Number Answer
> *ONE new element: the division doesn't come out evenly*

**Solve:** 2x + 5 = 16

### Sub-goal A: Isolate the term with x
2x + 5 = 16
2x + 5 - 5 = 16 - 5  ← Subtract 5 from both sides
2x = 11

### Sub-goal B: Solve for x
2x/2 = 11/2  ← Divide both sides by 2
x = 5.5

> **Don't panic!** The process is exactly the same. A decimal or fraction answer is perfectly valid.

### Check ✓
Substitute back: 2(5.5) + 5 = 11 + 5 = 16 ✓

# Solving Two-Step Linear Equations: Faded Worked Examples

## Overview for Teachers

Below are four worked examples applying **worked example fading**. Across the sequence, solution steps are progressively removed (marked with ✏️ YOUR TURN), requiring students to take increasing ownership of the procedure.

---

## The Six-Step Framework

| Step | Action                                                         |
|------|----------------------------------------------------------------|
| 1    | Write the equation and identify the two operations acting on x |
| 2    | Use the inverse operation to undo addition or subtraction      |
| 3    | Simplify both sides                                            |
| 4    | Use the inverse operation to undo multiplication or division   |
| 5    | Simplify to find the value of x                                |
| 6    | Check by substituting back into the original equation          |

---

## Example 1 — Fully Worked (All steps provided)

### Solve: 3x + 5 = 20

Step 1 – Identify: x is multiplied by 3, then 5 is added. Undo in reverse order.
Step 2 – Subtract 5 from both sides: 3x + 5 - 5 = 20 - 5
Step 3 – Simplify: 3x = 15
Step 4 – Divide both sides by 3: 3x/3 = 15/3
Step 5 – Simplify: x = 5
Step 6 – Check: 3(5) + 5 = 15 + 5 = 20 ✓

---

## Example 2 — Lightly Faded (Final two steps removed)

### Solve: 2x + 7 = 15

Step 1 – Identify: x is multiplied by 2, then 7 is added.
Step 2 – Subtract 7 from both sides: 2x + 7 - 7 = 15 - 7
Step 3 – Simplify: 2x = 8
Step 4 – Divide both sides by 2: 2x/2 = 8/2

✏️ YOUR TURN — Step 5: Simplify to find x.
x = _____

✏️ YOUR TURN — Step 6: Check by substituting your value back into 2x + 7 = 15.
2(___) + 7 = ___ + 7 = ___
Does it equal 15? ______

---

## Example 3 — Moderately Faded (Final four steps removed)

### Solve: 4x − 3 = 25

Step 1 – Identify: x is multiplied by 4, then 3 is subtracted.
Step 2 – Add 3 to both sides: 4x - 3 + 3 = 25 + 3

✏️ YOUR TURN — Step 3: Simplify both sides.
4x = _____

✏️ YOUR TURN — Step 4: What operation do you perform on both sides now? Do it.

✏️ YOUR TURN — Step 5: Simplify to find x.
x = _____

✏️ YOUR TURN — Step 6: Check by substituting back into 4x − 3 = 25.

✏️ YOUR TURN — Step 2: Perform the correct inverse operation on both sides.

✏️ YOUR TURN — Step 3: Simplify both sides.

## Teacher Notes

- **Why fading works:** Full worked examples reduce cognitive load for novices
  (Sweller & Cooper, 1985). Fading gradually shifts the cognitive work from
  the example to the learner (Renkl, Atkinson & Maier, 2002).
- **Step 1 is always provided** because identifying the structure is the
  conceptual anchor.
- **After Example 4**, students should be ready for fully independent practice.
- **Differentiation tip:** If a student struggles at Example 3, return them to
  Example 2 rather than jumping to Example 4.

Hello there! As a mathematics teacher, I always tell my Year 8 students that solving algebra equations is exactly like untying a knot or working backwards through a puzzle.

Our golden rule is the **Balance Method**: whatever you do to one side of the equals sign, you *must* do to the exact same thing to the other side. Our ultimate goal is always to **isolate the variable** (get the letter completely by itself).

To do this, we use **inverse (opposite) operations**. We also work in reverse order of operations (reverse BIDMAS/BODMAS) — meaning we generally undo addition and subtraction *before* we undo multiplication and division.

Here are 4 worked examples to show you exactly how it's done.

---

### Example 1: The standard addition equation
**Problem:** Solve 3x + 5 = 26

**Teacher's thought process:**
I want 'x' by itself. Right now, x is being multiplied by 3, and then 5 is being added to it. To untie this knot, I need to get rid of the +5 first, and then the ×3.

**Worked Solution:**
* Step 1: Undo the addition. Subtract 5 from both sides.
  3x + 5 - 5 = 26 - 5
  3x = 21
* Step 2: Undo the multiplication. Divide both sides by 3.
  3x/3 = 21/3
  x = 7

* Check your answer: 3(7) + 5 = 21 + 5 = 26. It works!

---

### Example 2: Dealing with subtraction
**Problem:** Solve 4y - 7 = 13

**Worked Solution:**
* Step 1: Undo the subtraction. Add 7 to both sides.
  4y - 7 + 7 = 13 + 7
  4y = 20
* Step 2: Undo the multiplication. Divide both sides by 4.
  4y/4 = 20/4
  y = 5

* Check your answer: 4(5) - 7 = 20 - 7 = 13. Perfect!

---

### Example 3: Equations with fractions (division)
**Problem:** Solve m/2 + 4 = 10

**Teacher's thought process:**
Don't let the fraction panic you! A fraction bar just means "divide". So, 'm' is being divided by 2, and then 4 is added.

**Worked Solution:**
* Step 1: Undo the addition. Subtract 4 from both sides.
  m/2 + 4 - 4 = 10 - 4
  m/2 = 6
* Step 2: Undo the division. Multiply both sides by 2.
  (m/2) × 2 = 6 × 2
  m = 12

* Check your answer: 12/2 + 4 = 6 + 4 = 10. Spot on!

---

### Example 4: The variable is on the right side
**Problem:** Solve 22 = 5p - 3

**Teacher's thought process:**
Students often freeze when the letter is on the right side. Don't worry! An equals sign is just a balance scale; it doesn't matter which side the letter is on.

**Worked Solution:**
* Step 1: Undo the subtraction. Add 3 to both sides.
  22 + 3 = 5p - 3 + 3
  25 = 5p
* Step 2: Undo the multiplication. Divide both sides by 5.
  25/5 = 5p/5
  5 = p
* Step 3: Rewrite for neatness.
  p = 5

* Check your answer: 5(5) - 3 = 25 - 3 = 22. Great job!

As an experienced mathematics teacher, I have designed these four worked examples specifically for Year 8 students.

To apply **Cognitive Load Theory (CLT)**, I have used the following principles:
* **The Worked Example Effect:** Providing step-by-step expert models to free up working memory.
* **The Split-Attention Effect:** Placing the explanatory text directly next to the mathematical steps (using a table format) so students don't have to look back and forth.
* **Signaling/Cueing:** Using bold text and consistent phrasing to draw attention to the most important information (the inverse operations).
* **Gradual Fading (Isolating Elements):** The examples gradually increase in complexity. We start with addition/multiplication, move to subtraction, introduce fractions (division), and finally include negative numbers.

---

### Teacher's Golden Rules for Solving Equations:
1. **Goal:** Get the letter (the variable) completely by itself.
2. **Balance:** Whatever you do to one side, you must do to the other.
3. **Reverse BIDMAS:** Undo addition/subtraction first, then undo multiplication/division second.

Hello! As an experienced mathematics teacher, I love using the **worked example fading effect**. It is a fantastic strategy from Cognitive Load Theory. By gradually removing steps (scaffolding), we prevent students from being overwhelmed by the whole process at once.

Here is a sequence of 4 examples designed for Year 8 students learning to solve two-step linear equations. We will use the **backward fading** technique, where the final steps are removed first.

---

### Topic: Solving Two-Step Linear Equations
**Goal:** Isolate the variable (get the letter by itself) by using inverse (opposite) operations.
**Rule:** Whatever you do to one side of the equals sign, you must do to the exact same to the other side!

---

#### Example 1: Fully Worked Example (Study this carefully)
**Solve for x: 4x + 7 = 23**

Teacher's thought process: I need to get x by itself. First, I will undo the addition. Then, I will undo the multiplication.

* Step 1: Subtract 7 from both sides to remove the constant.
  4x + 7 - 7 = 23 - 7
  4x = 16
* Step 2: Divide both sides by 4 to isolate x.
  4x/4 = 16/4
* Final Answer:
  x = 4

(Check: 4 × 4 + 7 = 16 + 7 = 23. It works!)

---

#### Example 2: Partially Faded (You do the last step)
**Solve for y: 5y - 3 = 22**

* Step 1: Add 3 to both sides.
  5y - 3 + 3 = 22 + 3
  5y = 25
* Step 2: Divide both sides by 5.
  5y/5 = 25/5
* Final Answer:
  y = _______

---

#### Example 3: Mostly Faded (You do the last two steps)
**Solve for m: 2m + 9 = 21**

* Step 1: Subtract 9 from both sides.
  2m = _______  (Write the new number on the right side)
* Step 2: __________________________ (What do you divide by?)
  2m/___  =  ___/___
* Final Answer:
  m = _______

---

#### Example 4: Fully Faded (Independent Practice)
**Solve for k: 6k - 5 = 19**

Teacher's thought process: You've got this! Apply the exact same pattern we used in the first three examples. Show all your working out.

* Step 1: __________________________

* Step 2: __________________________

* Final Answer:
  k = _______

(Don't forget to check your answer by putting it back into the original equation!)

---

### Teacher Notes on Implementation:
* **Spacing:** Ensure plenty of blank vertical space in Example 3 and Example 4.
* **Monitoring:** By the time they reach Example 3, you will quickly see who has grasped the concept.
* **Variability:** I alternated between addition (+7, +9) and subtraction (-3, -5) to ensure students don't blindly subtract in Step 1 every time.
